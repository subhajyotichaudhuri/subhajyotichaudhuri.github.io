About Me

I am an IIN Postdoctoral Fellow working with George C. Schatz in the Department of Chemistry at Northwestern University. I am developing computational methods combining ideas from combinatorics, graph theory, machine learning, and quantum chemistry to study electronic properties of complex systems. I am particularly interested in developing methods that allow us to get dynamical information like rates of charge, spin, and energy transfer in computationally challenging systems; with which I try to inverse-design materials with desired properties. Before this, I got my PhD with Victor S. Batista in the Department of Chemistry and the School of Engineering and Applied Science at Yale University working on developing methods for ab-initio prediction of electron transfer rates in weakly coupled systems.

Research

I am a theorist working on problems at the intersection of physics, chemistry, and engineering. I develop scalable mixed quantum-classical methods to study charge, spin, and energy transfer in non-equilibrium, driven, chiral, and complex systems; with the goal of understanding the multi-scale physics behind the electronic properties of materials. I work on a wide range of problems ranging from design of molecular catalysts for solar fuels applications to proteins for regenerative bioelectronics. I utilize advancements in the fields of mathematics (like algebraic topology and graph theory) and computer science (like computational geometry and manifold learning) to find analytical or numerical solutions for problems in chemical physics. I am also interested in developing interpretable machine-learning models for data-driven discovery of laws of physics for chemical systems. […more]

Impact

I am passionate about building a sustainable future, and work closely with some of the global leaders in the areas of photovoltaics, solar fuels catalysis, artificial photosynthesis, regenerative bioelectronics, metals separation etc. My aim is to develop scalable computational methods that go beyond the existing structure-function relationship regime and allow prediction of dynamical properties under realistic conditions; allowing us to truly perform in-silico design of functional materials. Some of the computational framework I develop benefits from using high performance computing facilities. Quantum computers now present a game-changing alternative in the simulation of quantum systems. A part of my research also focuses on developing methods compatible with quantum computers. I am currently working on understanding the design principles of molecules and materials for quantum sensing and transduction, and molecular qubits.
